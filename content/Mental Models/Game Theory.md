Game theory is the study of how humans behave in strategic situations. By ‘strategic’ we mean a situation in which **each person, when deciding what actions to take, must consider how others might respond to that action.**

 A **game** is defined to be any situation in which:

* There are at least two players (individuals, companies, nations, even species).
- Each player has a set of possible **strategies**—courses of action they can take.
- The strategies chosen by all players together determine the outcome.
- Each outcome comes with a set of **payoffs** (rewards), one for each player. Naturally, everyone wants the best payoff for themselves.

It's not about making a decision once, it's about but how people react when the same/similar situations **repeat** over and over again. Life is a loop of repeated interactions.

**Example:** Think about driving. By choosing to drive on the right side and follow rules, you cooperate—and everyone wins. If you defect and ignore the rules, chaos ensues.

In one experiment, scientists tested various strategies in a repeated 2-player game where both players can either cooperate or defect. 
The winning program—called **Tit-for-Tat**—followed four simple principles:

- **Be nice:** Start with cooperation.
- **Be retaliatory:** If the other defects, punish by defecting only once.
- **Be forgiving:** Don’t hold grudges—return to cooperation if the other does.
- **Be clear:** Make your behavior easy to predict.

**There’s a catch**: Tit for Tat assumed each player _always knows_ what the other did. In real life, we can misinterpret actions and things could easily spiral. The best solution was to occasionally **forgive an apparent defection**, just in case it was accidental.

**We see this in healthy relationships all the time: you don’t pounce on every slight. Maybe your friend was in a bad mood and snapped at you, if you snap back immediately, things escalate.** But if you overlook it once (“graciously ignore the first missile,” so to speak), they might realize their error and peace is kept.

**By viewing your choices through a game theory lens, you become more mindful of the consequences.** You start asking, “If I do this, how will others react? What chain of events am I setting in motion?”

In the end, l**ife isn’t about never losing a round**. Game theory reminds us that the real strategy is **staying in the game**. 

**TL;DR:** We naturally look out for ourselves. **_while selfishness can win one round, cooperation wins the final round._**

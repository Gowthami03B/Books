> **And then what??** 

First-order thinking is superficial and easy and just about everyone can do it (**a bad sign for anything involving an attempt at superiority**!)

Second-level thinking is **deep, complex and convoluted.**

I am hungry, let's eat a chocolate bar!! *solves the immediate problem without looking at the consequences.*

Second-order thinking is more deliberate. 
Needs thinking in terms of time. *What would happen every time you reach out for a chocolate bar when you are hungry?*

> **actions -> immediate effects (1st order) ->  effects of the effects (2nd order)** 

> *The road to out-thinking people can’t come from first-order thinking. It must come from second-order thinking. Extraordinary performance comes from seeing things that other people can’t see.*

![[Pasted image 20251012104835.png|250]]
The more **connected** an area space is, the more critical second-order thinking becomes as 
actions will have far reaching consequences. 

**Helps with**
- **Prioritizing long-term interests over immediate gains** 
	- Small losses/gains accumulated over long term will take you **towards** or **away** from desired future
	- Sometimes 1st order effects could be negative (investing in stocks at all time high, but 2nd order effects of compounding are still huge)
	- **Trust and trustworthiness** stem from multiple interactions. 2nd order thinking helps you understand the effects of your actions which underpins the **trust** you will build
- **Effective arguments**
	- More effective when we demonstrate that we considered the second-order effects and **put effort into verifying** that these are desirable as well.

**Ways to implement this** 
1. Always ask, **And then what?**
2. Think across time — What do the consequences look like in **10 minutes? 10 months? 10 Years?**  (have a journal and list the consequences)
3. Think how **different parts of the ecosystem** would respond. Say deciding what tech to use for a software app - clients impact, product impact, team impact etc

A lot of extraordinary things in life are the result of things that are ***first-order negative, second order positive.*** So just because things look like they have no immediate payoff, doesn’t mean that’s the case. 

> All it means is that you’ll have less competition if the second and third order consequences are positive because **everyone who thinks at the first order won’t think things through.**

Second-order thinking takes a lot of work. It’s not easy to think in terms of systems, interactions, and time. However, doing so is a smart way to separate yourself from the **masses**.

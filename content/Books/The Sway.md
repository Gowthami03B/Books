Ideas from the book "Sway - The Irresistible Pull of Irrational Behavior"

##### **Loss aversion** - our tendency to go to great lengths to avoid losses

- **Pain** associated with a loss is felt deeply than the joy of a gain
- The more there is on the line, the more loss averse/irrational we become 
- To withdraw immediately is accepting a sure loss. **We try to delay failure or loss as much as possible** . 
###### Overcoming the sway
- When things go wrong, we can either focus on short-term and apply a band-aid or understand that in the grand scheme of things this is a minor misstep. 
- **Having a long-term plan** and not casting it aside- is the key to deal with our fear of loss.
	*Small losses in the markets shouldn't hinder your long term goal of wealth accumulation*

##### **Commitment** - the force that makes us keep going though something is not working out
###### Overcoming the sway
- At one point, we gotta accept what's done is done and shift direction rather than dig deeper into the hole
##### **Value Attribution** - Our tendency to imbue a person/thing with certain qualities based on initial perceived value rather than objective data

* It acts as a quick mental shortcut to determine what's **worthy of our attention**
* Eg: When we get something at a discount, and our first experience was bad, we tend to think of it as low quality. 
* Once we attribute certain value for something, it's very difficult to view it in a different light
* You can take this to your advantage as well say at work - Eg: Simply being labeled however arbitrarily as having high leadership potential could translate into actual improved ability
* When we are **labeled** (say dependable), it's easy to start acting out as a way of being in the world. 
* **We try to fit into the mold created by the diagnosis.**
###### Overcoming the sway

- Be **mindful** and observe things as they are and not just for what they appear to be
*Sometimes we dismiss an item if the price is too low, instead ask yourself what if it was a gift instead.*
- It's impossible not to make judgements or be judged, but be mindful and prepare to change those judgements.

##### **Diagnosis Bias** - Our blindness to all evidence that contradicts the original assessment of a person/situation

- When we brand/label people, they take on the characteristics of the diagnosis
- Negative and external feelings about old age, can actually make people physically age faster
###### Overcoming the sway
- We can overcome this with **objective** thinking. 
- It's very easy to judge people based on their appearances etc and these constructs help us to quickly assess a situation. 
- But we have to be careful not to reply on too much on these judgements.
- Remain **flexible** and examine things from different perspectives. 
- Keep **evaluations tentative instead of certain**, learning to be comfortable with complex, sometimes contradictory info, and taking your time and considering things from different angles before coming to a conclusion.

##### Fairness 
- When we make decisions/take actions that affect others, keeping them **involved** will help ensure they feel the process is fair
- Voicing our own **discomfort/uncertainty** shifts the focus to the **situation** in hand - a divisive situation can be transformed into a collaborative effort, allowing people to feel that the process is fair.

##### **Pleasure center vs Altruism center**

- Studies showed that the pleasure center and altruism center cannot both function at the same time. *One of them are in control*. 
- Your coworker would be more likely to stay late and pitch in if you explained your predicament and asked for a favor, rather than offering 15$ for their time (which is too low)
* **Pleasure center has a way of sneaking up on us**. 
* Reward triggers an addictive response.
* Not only does our response to monetary reward resembles our response to cocaine, but so does our drive to attain the reward
* Also the **prospect of attaining a reward** excites the pleasure center more than the attainment of the reward itself

##### **Group setting**
- In a group setting, our **reasonableness** of our thinking can be distorted and compromised
* The person who speaks first can shift an entire group's opinion
* Supreme court judges use this - **Everyone speaks once before anybody speaks twice to ensure every opinion is presented**
* In a group, we worry that being odd man out will make others doubt our abilities, the challenge is to know when to **speak up**
* Although the sway of group conformity is strong, it depends on **unanimity** for it's power
* All it needs to break the sway of conformity is the presence of one more dissenting voice (doesn't matter if the dissenting voice is competent)

###### **Roles we play in a group setting**
- *initiator* : The person who always has ideas, likes to start projects, advocates for new ways of moving forward
- *blocker* : They question the merit or wisdom of new decisions. They put brakes and prevent the group from going down a dangerous path. It adds a perspective to question the status quo and evaluate pros and cons. To be blocked is frustrating, **but their opinions keeps the groups balanced**
- *supporter* : Either leans towards the initiator/blocker
- *observer* : neutral and comments on what's going on

We are all influenced by irrational psychological forces that undermine the **logical** perspective on the world around us. By recognizing these sways, we can hope to weaken the influence of these forces!!
